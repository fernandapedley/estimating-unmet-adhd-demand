This page presents the visualisations produced during the analysis of ADHD prevalence, referrals and waiting-list activity in England between December 2024 and December 2025.

The charts are presented in the same order as the exploratory analysis and illustrate the key patterns that informed the project's findings and recommendations.

---

## Chart 1. ADHD Prevalence and Waiting Lists Over Time

![ADHD Prevalence and Waiting Lists Over Time](images/chart_1_prevalence_waiting_list.png)

### What this chart shows

This chart compares recorded ADHD prevalence and waiting-list activity across the reporting period.

### Key observation

Both measures increased substantially between December 2024 and December 2025. A particularly noticeable increase occurred between January and February 2025, which may partly reflect improvements in reporting coverage associated with NHS England's ADHD Data Improvement Plan.

---

## Chart 2. ADHD Referrals Over Time

![ADHD Referrals Over Time](images/chart_2_referrals.png)

### What this chart shows

This chart shows trends in open referrals and new referrals across the reporting period.

### Key observation

Open referrals increased steadily throughout the year, while new referrals also showed continued growth. This suggests that demand for ADHD services remained high throughout the reporting period.

---

## Chart 3. ADHD Prevalence by Age Group

![ADHD Prevalence by Age Group](images/chart_3_prevalence_by_age.png)

### What this chart shows

This chart compares recorded ADHD prevalence across different age groups.

### Key observation

Adults aged 25+ consistently represented the largest proportion of recorded ADHD prevalence and accounted for most of the observed growth during the reporting period.

---

## Chart 4. ADHD Waiting Lists by Age Group

![ADHD Waiting Lists by Age Group](images/chart_4_waiting_list_by_age.png)

### What this chart shows

This chart compares waiting-list activity across age groups.

### Key observation

Waiting-list activity increased across all age groups but was most pronounced within adult services, particularly among people aged 25 and over.

---

## Chart 5. Waiting-List Demand Relative to ADHD Prevalence

![Waiting-List Demand Relative to ADHD Prevalence](images/chart_5_waiting_list_ratio.png)

### What this chart shows

This chart compares waiting-list activity relative to recorded ADHD prevalence across age groups over time.

### Key observation

Adult age groups consistently recorded substantially higher waiting-list-to-prevalence ratios than younger age groups. The gap remained remarkably stable throughout the reporting period.

---

## Chart 6. Average Waiting-List Demand Relative to ADHD Prevalence

![Average Waiting-List Demand Relative to ADHD Prevalence](images/chart_6_average_ratio.png)

### What this chart shows

This chart compares the average waiting-list-to-prevalence ratio for each age group across the reporting period.

### Key observation

Adults aged 25+ recorded the highest average ratio, followed closely by the 18–24 age group. This was one of the strongest findings identified during the analysis and suggests that service pressure may be concentrated within adult ADHD pathways.

---

## Chart 7. Potential Unmet Demand Indicator

![Potential Unmet Demand Indicator](images/chart_7_unmet_demand_indicator.png)

### What this chart shows

This chart presents a simple indicator created by comparing waiting-list activity with recorded ADHD prevalence across all age groups.

### Key observation

The indicator remained consistently high throughout the reporting period, suggesting that waiting-list activity represented a substantial proportion of recorded prevalence and providing a useful indication of ongoing service pressure.

---

# Summary

Taken together, the visualisations highlight several consistent patterns within the dataset:

* ADHD prevalence increased substantially during the reporting period.
* Waiting-list activity increased alongside prevalence.
* Adult age groups accounted for most of the observed growth.
* Adult services experienced consistently higher service pressure than children's services.
* Potential unmet demand appears greatest within adult ADHD pathways.

These findings informed the conclusions and recommendations presented within the final capstone report.
