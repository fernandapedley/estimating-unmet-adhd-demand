## Overview

This page presents the visualisations produced during the analysis of ADHD prevalence, referrals and waiting-list activity in England between December 2024 and December 2025.

The charts are presented in the same order as the exploratory analysis and illustrate the key patterns that informed the project's findings and recommendations.

---

# 6.1 ADHD Prevalence and Waiting Lists Over Time

![ADHD Prevalence and Waiting Lists Over Time](images/chart_1_prevalence_waiting_list.png)

### Interpretation

The first chart compares ADHD prevalence and waiting-list activity across the reporting period. Both measures increased substantially between December 2024 and December 2025.

One of the most noticeable changes occurred between January and February 2025. Based on NHS England documentation, this may partly reflect improvements in reporting coverage associated with the ADHD Data Improvement Plan.

The chart suggests that increases in ADHD prevalence were accompanied by similar increases in waiting-list activity, indicating growing demand across ADHD services.

---

# 6.2 ADHD Referrals Over Time

![ADHD Referrals Over Time](images/chart_2_referrals.png)

### Interpretation

This chart explores trends in open referrals and new referrals across the reporting period.

Open referrals increased steadily throughout the year, while new referrals also showed continued growth. This suggests that demand for ADHD services remained high throughout the reporting period.

The increase in open referrals alongside continued growth in new referrals may suggest that demand was entering the system faster than services were able to fully process it. However, additional operational data would be required to confirm this interpretation.

---

# 6.3 ADHD Prevalence by Age Group

![ADHD Prevalence by Age Group](images/chart_3_prevalence_by_age.png)

### Interpretation

The age-group analysis showed that adults aged 25+ consistently represented the largest proportion of recorded ADHD prevalence throughout the reporting period.

The most notable increase occurred between January and February 2025, after which prevalence continued to rise more gradually across the remainder of the year. This pattern mirrors the increase observed in the overall prevalence analysis.

Although prevalence increased across all age groups, the largest absolute growth was observed within the adult age groups, particularly those aged 25 and over. This suggests that adults were the main contributors to the overall increase in recorded ADHD prevalence during the reporting period.

---

# 6.4 ADHD Waiting Lists by Age Group

![ADHD Waiting Lists by Age Group](images/chart_4_waiting_list_by_age.png)

### Interpretation

A similar pattern was observed when examining waiting-list activity by age group.

Adults aged 25+ consistently recorded the largest waiting lists throughout the reporting period. Waiting lists also increased substantially within the 18–24 age group, while the 5–17 age group experienced more modest growth.

These findings suggest that much of the increase in waiting-list demand was concentrated within adult ADHD services. This aligns with the prevalence analysis and indicates that rising demand may be affecting adult pathways more significantly than children's services.

---

# 6.5 Waiting-List Demand Relative to ADHD Prevalence

![Waiting-List Demand Relative to ADHD Prevalence](images/chart_5_waiting_list_ratio.png)

### Interpretation

This chart compares waiting-list activity relative to recorded ADHD prevalence across age groups over time.

Adult age groups consistently recorded substantially higher waiting-list-to-prevalence ratios than younger age groups. The gap remained remarkably stable throughout the reporting period.

This suggests that the pattern is unlikely to be explained by a single reporting month, data anomaly or short-term fluctuation. Instead, it appears to represent a more sustained difference in service demand and service pressure between adult and children's ADHD pathways.

---

# 6.6 Average Waiting-List Demand Relative to ADHD Prevalence

![Average Waiting-List Demand Relative to ADHD Prevalence](images/chart_6_average_ratio.png)

### Interpretation

The average waiting-list-to-prevalence ratio was calculated to provide a simpler comparison between age groups.

Adults aged 25+ recorded the highest average ratio, followed closely by the 18–24 age group. Younger age groups showed substantially lower ratios.

This reinforces the earlier finding that adult ADHD services may be experiencing greater relative service pressure. The relatively small differences observed between the adult age groups suggest that increased service pressure may not be limited to a single adult cohort but may instead affect adult ADHD pathways more broadly.

---

# 6.7 Potential Unmet Demand Indicator

![Potential Unmet Demand Indicator](images/chart_7_unmet_demand_indicator.png)

### Interpretation

Finally, a simple indicator was created by comparing waiting-list activity with recorded ADHD prevalence across all age groups.

The indicator remained consistently high throughout the reporting period, suggesting that waiting-list activity represented a substantial proportion of recorded prevalence.

While this should not be interpreted as a direct measure of unmet need, it provides a useful indication of service pressure and highlights the relationship between prevalence and waiting-list demand.

---

# Exploratory Analysis Summary

The exploratory analysis identified several consistent patterns across the dataset.

* ADHD prevalence increased substantially during the reporting period.
* Waiting-list activity increased alongside prevalence.
* Referral volumes remained high throughout the year.
* Adult age groups accounted for most of the observed growth.
* Adult services consistently showed higher waiting-list demand relative to recorded prevalence than children's services.
* The difference between adult and children's services remained remarkably stable throughout the reporting period.

Taken together, these findings suggest that service pressure may be concentrated within adult ADHD pathways and provide a useful starting point for exploring potential unmet demand.

These observations informed the more detailed findings, conclusions and recommendations presented within the final capstone report.
