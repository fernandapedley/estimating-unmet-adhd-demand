This page summarises the principal findings from the analysis of ADHD prevalence, referrals and waiting-list activity in England between December 2024 and December 2025.

## Summary of Findings

| Finding                  | Observation                                                                     |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| ADHD prevalence          | Increased by approximately 54%                                                  |
| Waiting-list activity    | Increased by approximately 54%                                                  |
| Open referrals           | Increased by more than 50%                                                      |
| Largest growth           | Adults aged 25+                                                                 |
| Highest service pressure | Adults aged 18–24 and 25+                                                       |
| Key conclusion           | Adult ADHD pathways appear to experience the greatest relative service pressure |

---

## 1. ADHD Prevalence Increased Substantially

![ADHD Prevalence and Waiting Lists Over Time](images/chart_1_prevalence_waiting_list.png)

### Finding

Recorded ADHD prevalence increased from approximately 366,000 people in December 2024 to more than 562,000 people by December 2025, representing growth of around 54%.

### Why it matters

The increase suggests that growing numbers of people are being identified and supported by ADHD services. However, part of this increase may also reflect improvements in reporting coverage associated with NHS England's ADHD Data Improvement Plan.

The increase was particularly noticeable between January and February 2025, after which prevalence continued to rise throughout the remainder of the year.

---

## 2. Waiting-List Activity and Referrals Continued to Increase

![ADHD Referrals Over Time](images/chart_2_referrals.png)

### Finding

Waiting-list activity increased from approximately 258,000 people to more than 398,000 people during the reporting period, while open referrals increased by more than 50%. New referrals also showed a smaller but still notable increase.

### Why it matters

These findings suggest that demand for ADHD assessment and treatment remained high throughout the year. Services were not only receiving new referrals but were also continuing to manage large numbers of people already waiting within the pathway.

The similarity between growth in prevalence, waiting-list activity and open referrals suggests that increasing identification of ADHD was accompanied by increasing demand for assessment and treatment services.

---

## 3. Adult Age Groups Accounted for Most of the Observed Growth

![ADHD Prevalence by Age Group](images/chart_3_prevalence_by_age.png)

### Finding

Adults aged 25 and over consistently represented the largest proportion of recorded ADHD prevalence and accounted for most of the observed growth during the reporting period.

The 18–24 age group also experienced substantial increases, while younger age groups showed more modest growth.

### Why it matters

Although prevalence increased across all age groups, the largest increases occurred within adult services. This suggests that rising demand may be particularly concentrated within adult ADHD pathways.

---

## 4. Adult Services Experienced Higher Levels of Relative Service Pressure

![Waiting-List Demand Relative to ADHD Prevalence](images/chart_5_waiting_list_ratio.png)

### Finding

When waiting-list activity was compared with recorded ADHD prevalence, adults aged 18–24 and 25+ consistently showed substantially higher waiting-list-to-prevalence ratios than younger age groups.

The pattern remained remarkably stable throughout the reporting period.

### Why it matters

The stability of this pattern suggests that it is unlikely to be explained by a single reporting month, short-term fluctuation or isolated data anomaly.

The findings indicate that adult ADHD services may be experiencing greater relative service pressure than children's services and may reflect a mismatch between growing demand and available service capacity within adult pathways.

---

## 5. Average Service Pressure Was Highest in Adult Age Groups

![Average Waiting-List Demand Relative to ADHD Prevalence](images/chart_6_average_ratio.png)

### Finding

Adults aged 25+ recorded the highest average waiting-list-to-prevalence ratio, followed closely by the 18–24 age group. Younger age groups showed substantially lower ratios.

### Why it matters

This reinforces the finding that adult ADHD services may be experiencing greater relative service pressure than children's services.

The relatively small difference between the adult age groups suggests that increased service pressure may not be limited to a single adult cohort but may instead affect adult ADHD pathways more broadly.

---

## 6. Potential Unmet Demand May Be Greatest Within Adult ADHD Services

![Potential Unmet Demand Indicator](images/chart_7_unmet_demand_indicator.png)

### Finding

A simple indicator comparing waiting-list activity with recorded ADHD prevalence remained consistently high throughout the reporting period.

Across multiple analyses, adult age groups consistently showed the highest levels of waiting-list demand relative to recorded ADHD prevalence.

### Why it matters

This indicator should not be interpreted as a direct measure of unmet need. Instead, it was used as a proxy measure of service pressure and to explore where potential unmet demand may exist.

While further investigation would be required to understand the causes of the observed patterns, the findings suggest that potential unmet demand may be greatest within adult ADHD services.

---

## Overall Conclusion

The analysis identified several consistent patterns across the ADHD dataset between December 2024 and December 2025.

Recorded ADHD prevalence, waiting-list activity and referral volumes all increased during the reporting period. Adult age groups, particularly those aged 18–24 and 25+, accounted for most of the observed growth and consistently showed higher waiting-list demand relative to recorded ADHD prevalence than younger age groups.

The project was not able to measure unmet need directly and therefore cannot determine how many people may require assessment but have not yet entered ADHD pathways. However, the findings provide a useful indication of where service pressure appears to be concentrated.

Taken together, the results suggest that adult ADHD services may be experiencing disproportionately higher levels of demand relative to the size of the known ADHD population and highlight areas that may warrant further investigation, service development and future resource planning.
