This page summarises the principal findings from the analysis of ADHD prevalence, referrals and waiting-list activity in England between December 2024 and December 2025.

---

## Summary of Findings

| Finding                  | Observation                                                            |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| ADHD prevalence          | Increased by approximately 54%                                         |
| Waiting-list activity    | Increased by approximately 54%                                         |
| Open referrals           | Increased by more than 50%                                             |
| Largest growth           | Adults aged 25+                                                        |
| Highest service pressure | Adults aged 18–24 and 25+                                              |
| Key conclusion           | Adult ADHD pathways appear to experience the greatest service pressure |

---

## 1. ADHD Prevalence Increased Substantially

![Prevalence and Waiting List Trends](images/chart_1_prevalence_waiting_list.png)

### Finding

Recorded ADHD prevalence increased from approximately 366,000 people in December 2024 to more than 562,000 people by December 2025, representing growth of around 54%.

### Why it matters

The increase suggests that growing numbers of people are being identified and supported by ADHD services. Waiting-list activity increased by a similar proportion, indicating that growth in recorded prevalence was accompanied by increasing demand for assessment and treatment services.

---

## 2. Referral Activity Remained High Throughout the Reporting Period

![Referral Trends](images/chart_2_referrals.png)

### Finding

Open referrals increased steadily throughout the reporting period, while new referrals also continued to rise.

### Why it matters

This suggests that ADHD services were continuing to receive new demand while simultaneously managing large numbers of people already within the referral pathway. Together, these trends indicate sustained pressure on services throughout the year.

---

## 3. Adult Age Groups Accounted for Most of the Observed Growth

![Prevalence by Age Group](images/chart_3_prevalence_by_age.png)

### Finding

Adults aged 25 and over consistently represented the largest proportion of recorded ADHD prevalence and accounted for most of the observed growth during the reporting period.

### Why it matters

Although prevalence increased across all age groups, the largest increases occurred within adult services. This suggests that rising demand may be particularly concentrated within adult ADHD pathways.

---

## 4. Adult Services Experienced Higher Levels of Service Pressure

![Waiting List Ratio](images/chart_5_waiting_list_ratio.png)

### Finding

Adult age groups consistently showed higher waiting-list demand relative to recorded prevalence than younger age groups.

### Why it matters

The pattern remained remarkably stable throughout the reporting period, suggesting that the difference is unlikely to be explained by short-term fluctuations. This may indicate a mismatch between growing demand and available service capacity within adult ADHD pathways.

---

## 5. Potential Unmet Demand May Be Concentrated Within Adult Pathways

![Potential Unmet Demand Indicator](images/chart_7_unmet_demand_indicator.png)

### Finding

The potential unmet demand indicator remained consistently high throughout the reporting period.

### Why it matters

Although the indicator should not be interpreted as a direct measure of unmet need, it provides a useful indication of service pressure and highlights areas that may warrant further investigation. Across multiple analyses, adult age groups consistently showed the highest levels of relative demand.

---

# Overall Conclusion

Taken together, the findings suggest that ADHD demand continued to grow across England during the reporting period and that adult services may be experiencing disproportionately higher levels of pressure than children's services.

While the project was not able to measure unmet need directly, the analysis provides a useful indication of where service pressure appears to be concentrated and highlights opportunities for further investigation and service improvement.
